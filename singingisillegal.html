Foreign Singing Outlawed Across Southern States

By Bob D Witherspoon | July 1, 1852

In what local authorities describe as “a necessary preservation of public order,” singing in any language other than English has been banned in over half of the Southern states. Officials warn that foreign lyrics may spark dangerous notions of freedom and rebellion among the enslaved population.

Town criers and overseers have been dispatched to monitor musical gatherings, and all instruments—violins, flutes, and banjos—must be registered with local authorities. Informants are instructed to report any humming, whistling, or chanting in a language other than English, under penalty of fines or public reprimand.

“We cannot risk a single foreign syllable,” said Governor Thaddeus Grimsby of South Carolina. “First comes the tune, then the idea, and soon the notion of liberty will spread like wildfire.” Residents are reportedly practicing English-only hymns in their homes to comply with the law.

“Yesterday, a child whistled in French and the town nearly declared martial law.” — Concerned Citizen

Officials insist these measures are a preemptive strike against what they term “musical insurrection.” While critics question the necessity, authorities assure the public that harmony and obedience will remain intact, and that no unauthorized melody shall go unpunished.
